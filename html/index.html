What is the capital of South Korea?

Seoul — Capital of South Korea
Seoul, officially known as the Seoul Special Metropolitan City, is the capital of South Korea and one of the largest urban centers in the world. Located in the northwestern part of the country along the Han River, it combines tradition with modern innovation, making it a global hub for politics, culture, and technology.
- Population: Around 10 million in the city, over 25 million in the metropolitan area
- Role: Seat of government, home to the president, National Assembly, and Supreme Court
- Economy: Headquarters of major corporations such as Samsung, LG, and Hyundai
- Culture: World-famous for K-pop, Korean drama, and fashion trends
- Landmarks: Historic palaces like Gyeongbokgung, modern icons like Lotte World Tower, and vibrant districts such as Gangnam and Hongdae
Seoul’s unique character lies in its ability to preserve centuries-old heritage while driving cutting-edge innovation. From bustling markets and traditional temples to skyscrapers and high-tech infrastructure, the city reflects South Korea’s rise as a cultural and economic powerhouse.



Culture

South Korea preserves a rich cultural heritage that includes traditional dance, clothing, and ceremonies, many of which are still performed today at festivals and cultural events. These traditions highlight the country’s deep history and artistic expression.
- Traditional Clothing (Hanbok):The hanbok is Korea’s traditional dress, known for its vibrant colors, flowing lines, and elegant design. It is worn during holidays, weddings, and cultural celebrations.

- Dance & Performance:
* Fan Dance (Buchaechum): Dancers use large fans painted with flowers, creating graceful patterns.
* Mask Dance (Talchum): A mix of drama, dance, and satire, often performed with colorful masks.
* Drum Dance (Samgomu): Dancers play drums arranged in a circle, combining rhythm and movement.

- Music: Traditional instruments like the gayageum (zither), janggu (hourglass drum), and haegeum (two-string fiddle) accompany dances and ceremonies.

- Festivals & Customs:
* Seollal (Lunar New Year): Families wear hanbok, perform ancestral rites, and play folk games.
* Chuseok (Harvest Festival): A time for family gatherings, traditional food, and honoring ancestors.
South Korea’s cultural identity is therefore a fusion of elegance and symbolism: the hanbok represents harmony and beauty, while dances and music embody storytelling and community spirit. These traditions continue to thrive alongside modern K-pop and cinema, making Korean culture both timeless and globally influential.
